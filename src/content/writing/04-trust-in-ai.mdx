Users don't trust AI. They trust the experience of using an AI-powered product.

This is an important distinction. The trustworthiness of the model — its accuracy, its consistency, its reasoning quality — is table stakes. It's necessary but not sufficient. Users don't have direct access to model quality. They experience it through interface.

Which means the interface is where trust is built or destroyed.

## What trust actually requires

In physical product design, trust is often studied through the lens of reliability: does the product do what it says it will do, consistently? That's still necessary for AI products. But there are additional dimensions.

**Predictability.** Users need to be able to form accurate expectations about what the AI will do. Not because the AI needs to behave simply — but because users need to be able to update their mental model through experience. An AI that surprises users constantly, even with good surprises, fails to build the kind of trust that leads to habitual use.

**Transparency.** Users need to know enough about what the AI did and why to evaluate the output. Not technical transparency — behavioral transparency. "Here's what I found, and here's why I prioritized it this way" builds trust. A result with no explanation requires blind faith.

**Correctability.** Users need to be able to fix the AI when it's wrong — easily, quickly, without losing their work. An uncorrectable AI is a liability. Users will stop using features where mistakes are expensive.

**Consistency.** The interface around AI needs to behave consistently, even when the model's output varies. If the same action produces different interface states on different days, users lose their model of how the product works.

## Anti-patterns I see repeatedly

**False confidence.** Presenting uncertain outputs with the same visual weight as high-confidence ones. Users can't calibrate if they can't tell when the AI is guessing.

**Buried undo.** Making it hard to reverse AI actions. Users will limit their use of AI features to low-stakes tasks if reversal is painful.

**All-or-nothing delegation.** Requiring users to either fully trust the AI or do the work themselves. No middle ground. This leaves a huge portion of users underserved.

**Opaque failure modes.** Telling users "something went wrong" without any indication of what, why, or what they can do. This is the AI equivalent of a null pointer exception showing up in the UI.

**Inconsistent personality.** AI that sounds confident in some contexts and hedging in others, for no apparent reason. Users lose their sense of what they're dealing with.

## What good looks like

The AI products I trust the most share a few properties:

They tell me what they're confident about and what they're not. They make it easy to undo anything they do. They give me enough context about their output that I can evaluate it rather than just accept it. They behave the same way every time I use them. They improve predictably as I use them more.

None of these are model properties. They're all interface decisions.

The model is the engine. The interface is the car. Nobody talks about trusting the engine.
