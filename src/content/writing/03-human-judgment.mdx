There's a version of the "AI will replace designers" argument that I find both boring and wrong. Boring because it's the same argument that's been made about every productivity-increasing technology. Wrong because it misidentifies where the value actually lives.

The interesting question isn't "will AI replace designers?" It's "what does the designer role look like when AI handles the execution?"

My answer: it looks like a lot more judgment, a lot less production.

## What AI handles well

The list is getting longer. As of 2025, AI handles well:

- Generating code from well-specified design intent
- Writing documentation from structured input
- Producing first-draft copy for review
- Resizing, adapting, and exporting design assets
- Catching obvious consistency violations in design files
- Running accessibility checks
- Generating test cases from component specifications

This is a significant portion of what design and front-end work used to require full-time specialist attention. The production layer is compressing.

## What judgment still owns

**Goal framing.** Before any work starts, someone has to answer: what is this for? Who is it for? What problem does it solve? What would success look like? AI can help you answer these questions, but it can't ask them on your behalf. The framing is the most important work, and framing is judgment.

**Constraint navigation.** Real product work happens inside constraints: technical, organizational, legal, budgetary, competitive. AI doesn't know your constraints unless you tell it. And knowing which constraints are real versus negotiable — that's judgment accumulated from experience, not something you can prompt.

**Quality standards.** What does "good" look like for this project? What's the bar? Who's setting it? This is a judgment call that requires knowing the context, the users, the competitive landscape, and the organization's values. AI can execute to a quality bar you define. It can't define the bar.

**Consistency over time.** Design systems and product experiences accumulate debt when small decisions are made without reference to the bigger picture. Knowing when a component is subtly wrong — not because it breaks any rule, but because it's drifting from the spirit of the system — requires having carried the system in your head for long enough to feel the drift. That's a human skill.

**The call.** At some point, someone decides what ships. That person owns the consequences of the decision. That ownership is fundamental — it's what makes judgment meaningful. AI can inform the decision. It can't bear the responsibility.

## The strategic shift

When AI handles production work, the value of good judgment increases. Not because judgment was less valuable before — it was always the highest-leverage part of the work — but because the ratio shifts.

In a world where production is cheap, the bottleneck moves to the quality of the intent and the precision of the evaluation. Both of those are judgment work.

The designers who will be most valuable in the next era aren't the ones who can produce the most — it's those who can direct the most, with the highest precision and the clearest standards.

The skill to develop is not output. It's judgment, articulated clearly enough to direct a production system.
